\section{Languages Focus}

\subsection{Present simple (I do)}
\index{Present simple}
Use the simple for things in general or things that happen repeatedly. 
In many languages, the present simple is used to describe what is happening now. 
Notice that this is not the case in English. \\
- to talk about \underline{things which are always true}:\\
\textit{Water \textbf{boils} at 100° centigrade.}\\
\textit{He \textbf{has} blue eyes.}\\
- to talk about \underline{habits and routines}:\\
\textit{He always \textbf{goes} to bed before midnight.}\\
\textit{What \textbf{do} you \textbf{do}? (What is your job?)}

\subsection{Present continuous (I am doing)}
\index{Present continuous}
Use the continuous for something that \underline{is happening at} or \underline{around the time of
speaking}. 
The action is \underline{not finished.} \\
\textit{The water \textbf{is boiling}. Can you turn it off?}\\
- Use the continuous for a \underline{temporary situation}\\
\textit{You \textbf{are working} hard today.}
\textit{I hear you have a new job. How \textbf{are} you \textbf{getting} on?}\\
\textit{What \textbf{are} you do\textbf{ing} (now)?}\\

\subsection{\textit{In case}}
\textit{In case} is used when we want to be prepared for \underline{something that may happen}. 
\textit{In case} is \underline{follow by the Present simple} tense but refers to future or present
time.\\
\textit{Take an anorak\textbf{ in case it rains}.}\\
\textit{In case} \underline{can also be used with the Past tense}:\\
\textit{I took an overcoat \textbf{in case it was cold}.}\\

You need a modal verb for a future form (\textit{will}), but you need
auxiliary verbs for other time forms (\textit{be, have}).  \\\\

\section{Languages Focus}

\subsection{\textit{To be used to + -ing + noun}}
This form is used to indicate how a person feels about something or about
doing something. 
It express \underline{how comfortable or familiar a thing or action is} to them.\\
\textit{Pablo \textbf{is used to} eat\textbf{ing} later in the evening.}\\
\textit{I \textbf{am used to the cold}.}

\subsection{\textit{To get used to}}
This shows a \underline{change in the how we feel} about something:\\
\textit{I \textbf{can't get used to} this weather.} 
(I'm not able to change my feelings.)\\
\textit{I am \textbf{getting used to} eat\textbf{ing} later.} 
(My feelings about it are changing.)\\
\textit{I've \textbf{got used to} drink\textbf{ing} this beer.} 
(My feelings have now changed.)\\

\subsection{Present perfect (I have done)}
This tense is formed by: \textit{Have} + the past participle of the verb.\\
- to talk about past \underline{experiences in your life}:\\
\textit{I\textbf{'ve been} to Egypt three times.}\\
- to talk about things which have \underline{happened in unfinished periods of time}:\\
\textit{I\textbf{'ve drunk} too much coffee today.}\\
\textit{I\textbf{'ve learnt} how to use a PC.}\\
- to talk about things which have \underline{recently happened}:\\
\textit{She\textbf{'s just written} a letter to him.} = 
\textit{She \textbf{has just written} a letter to him.}\\

\subsection{Present perfect vs Past simple}
If you need to decide which tense to use, ask yourself the question
\underline{When}?\\
\textit{I\textbf{'ve never been} to Italy, but I\textbf{ went} to Greece last summer.}\\
\textit{I\textbf{'ve never been} to Italy, ...} 
\underline{When?} In my life (Present perfect).\\
\textit{... but I\textbf{ went} to Greece last summer.} 
\underline{When?} Last summer (Past simple).\\\\
The \underline{Past simple} is used to talk about things which \underline{happened in periods which
have finished}.\\

\subsection{Comparison of adjectives}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
One-syllable adjectives            & two-syllable adjectives          & three or more syllable adjectives\\ \hline
calm - calm\textbf{er}             & tidy - tid\textbf{ier}           & enthusiastic - \textbf{more} enthusiastic\\
old - old\textbf{er}               & dirty - dirt\textbf{ier}         & responsible - \textbf{more} responsible\\   
hot - hot\underline{t}\textbf{er}  & helpful - \textbf{more} helpful  & \\
wet - wet\underline{t}\textbf{er}  & handsome - \textbf{more} handsome& \\
\end{tabular}
Exceptions:\\
good - better / (best)\\
bad - worse / (worst)\\
far - further / (farther): \textit{Tell me if you need any \textbf{further} information.}\\\\
\underline{One-syllable adjectives} form the comparative by \underline{adding \textit{er}}.\\
\underline{Single consonants at the end} of the word \underline{double} before adding \textit{er}.\\
\underline{Two-syllable adjectives ending in \textit{y}} from the comparative by \underline{changing \textit{y} to \textit{i}
and adding \textit{er}.}\\
\underline{Two-syllable adjectives ending in other letters and all adjectives of three
ore more syllable} form the comparative by \underline{adding \textit{more}}.\\\\
\underline{Positive comparisons} are made with \underline{\textit{than}}:\\
\textit{John is old\textbf{er than} Mary.}\\\\
\underline{Comparisons of equality} are made with \underline{\textit{as ... as}}.:\\
\textit{John is \textbf{as} tall \textbf{as} Mary.}\\\\
\underline{\textit{The same} + noun + \textit{as}}:\\
\textit{Jim is \textit{the \textbf{same} height \textbf{as}} Sue.}\\\\
\underline{Negative comparisons} are made with \underline{\textit{not as ... as}}:\\
\textit{This baby is \textbf{not as} young \textbf{as} that one.}\\\\
\textit{Not as ... as} is possible with all adjectives. \\
\underline{\textit{Less than}} is usually found \underline{in more formal language}.

\subsection{Superlatives}
The superlative is formed by adding \textit{est} to one-syllable adjectives, \textit{iest} to
two-syllable adjectives ending in \textit{y} and \textit{most} before longer adjectives:\\\\
\textit{It's the old\textbf{est} building in the city.}\\
\textit{It was the prett\textbf{iest} village I had ever visited.}\\
\textit{He is the \textbf{most} enthusiastic student in the class.}\\\\
Exceptions:\\
good - (better) / best\\
bad - (worse) / worst\\
far - (further) / farther:

\subsection{Causative \textit{have}}
This form exists in a variety of tenses and in each case it is the verb 'to
have' which indicates the tense. 
The \underline{verb} indicating the action \underline{is always the past participle}.\\
\textit{I'm \textbf{having my} car repair\textbf{ed.}} 
(Someone is repairing my car.)\\
Compare with: \textit{I'm repairing my car.} 
(This means I am repairing it myself.)\\\\
Causative \textit{have} is formed by:\\
1) The subject = \textit{Sharon}\\
2) The verb to have = \textit{is having}\\
3) The object = \textit{her central heating}\\
4) The past participle of the main verb = \textit{serviced.}\\
\textit{Sharon is having her central heating serviced.}

\subsection{Genitive \textit{'s}}
The genitive \textit{'s} is mainly used to \underline{indicate possession} of things by people, and
family relationships. 
\underline{Add \textit{'s} to singular nouns.}\\
\textit{Sally\textbf{'s} dog.}\\
\textit{Sally and Peter\textbf{'s} dog.}\\
\textit{Nick\textbf{'s} brother.}\\\\
\underline{Add \textit{'} only to plural nouns endings in \textit{s}.}\\
\textit{The girl\textbf{s'} books.}\\
\textit{My grandparent\textbf{s'} house.}\\\\
Plural nouns not ending in \textit{s} are an exception:\\
\textit{The children\textbf{'s} books.}\\\\
\underline{Animals, days and cities} sometimes take the genitive.
Most other objects do not:\\
\textit{The \textbf{dog's} basket.}\\
\textit{\textbf{Monday's} lesson.}\\
\textit{\textbf{London's} theatres.}\\
but\\
\textit{The leg of the table.}\\
\textit{The chair leg.}\\

\subsection{\textit{Needs doing}}
This form is used to indicate that \underline{a certain job should be done}, but we do not
know, or do not want to know, who is going to do the job:\\
\textit{The windows \textbf{need cleaning}.} (They are dirty.)\\
\textit{The tape recorder \textbf{needs mending}.} (It is broken.)\\

\subsection{\textit{For/Since}}
\textit{For} and \textit{since} are used with perfect tenses to \underline{indicate length of time}.\\
\underline{\textit{Since}} can also be used with the Past simple.\\
\textit{Since refers to a date in the past} and is used to talk about things happening
between then and now.\\
\underline{\textit{For} refers to the period of time} that has passed between a point of time in
the past and now.\\
\textit{I've known Mary \textbf{since March}.}\\
\textit{I've known Mary \textbf{for three months}.} (It is now June.)\\\\
\textit{Since} can also be used in the middle of sentences which begin with a time period:\\
\textit{\textbf{It's a long time since} I've seen you.} (I haven't seen you for a long time).\\\\
\textit{Since} + Past simple:\\
\textit{\textbf{It's two years since} I left the army.} (I left the army two years ago - finished action in the past.)\\

\subsection{Linking words}
\subsubsection{\textit{After/After that}}
\underline{\textit{After}} is generally \underline{followed by a noun, gerund or phrase}:\\
\textit{\textbf{After coffee} we went home.}\\
\textit{\textbf{After} drink\textbf{ing} our coffee we went home.}\\
\textit{\textbf{After we had drunk our coffee} we went home.}\\
When \textit{after} is used at the beginning of a sentence, we are saying that the
events happened in the order which they are mentioned.\\
\underline{\textit{After} + first event + second event.}
\subsubsection{\textit{After that/Afterwards}}
These refer to something already mentioned.\\
\underline{First event + \textit{afterwards} + second event}:\\
\textit{We had coffee. \textbf{After that} we went home.}\\
\textit{\textbf{Afterwards} we went home.}
\subsubsection{\textit{Before}}
\underline{\textit{Before}} follow the same rules as \textit{after} when it is at the
beginning. \\ 
\underline{It can be followed by a noun, gerund or phrase.}\\
When \textit{before} is used at the beginning of a sentence it indicates that two
events which are mentioned are in reverse order:\\
\underline{\textit{Before }+ second event + first event}:\\
\textit{\textbf{Before} I \textbf{paid} the bill I \textbf{checked} the total to make sure it was correct.}\\
However, when \textit{before} is used between two events the order is:\\
\underline{First event + \textit{before} + second event}:\\
\textit{I checked my change \textbf{before} I left the shop.}
\subsubsection{\textit{Although/Despite}}
These are used \underline{to indicate contrast}.\\
\underline{\textit{Although} is followed by a subject and verb}:\\
\textit{\textbf{Although} there was  very little snow, the skiing resort was crowded.}\\
\underline{\textit{Despite} is followed by a noun, a noun phrase or gerund + gerund + verb}:\\
\textit{\textbf{Despite the fact that} it was cold, the central heating was turned off.}\\
\textit{\textbf{Despite the cold weather}, we decided to climb the mountain.}\\
\textit{\textbf{Despite} feel\textbf{ing cold}, we decided to climb the mountain.}\\
We can also say \textit{even though (although)} --- \textit{in spite of
  (despite)}\\
\underline{Although ... = Despite the fact ...} 
\subsection{\textit{Used to} + infinitive}
This tells us about things that happened in the past but which \underline{don't happen
now}. These are \underline{things which happened regulary over a long period of time}.\\
\textit{He \textbf{used to play football} when he was a child.}\\
Does he play football now? (no) --- Did he play football often\ (yes)\\
\underline{\textit{Used to} does not exist in the present.} 
The present \underline{equivalent} of \textit{used to} is \underline{\textit{usually}}.\\
\subsection{Past perfect}
When we tell a story, we set a time in the past in which the narrative takes
places. When we refer to actions and periods before that, the Past perfect is
used:\\
\textit{When we got back to the house, \textbf{he had} remov\textbf{ed} the furniture.}\\
(First he removed the furniture, then we got back.)\\
\textit{When we got back to the house, \textbf{he} remov\textbf{ed} the furniture.}\\
(First we got back, then he removed the furniture.)\\

\subsection{Relative Clauses}
Relative Clauses can be "defining" or "non-defining". \\
They \underline{begin with a relative pronoun} --- \textit{\textbf{who}} or \textit{\textbf{that}} used for people, and \textit{\textbf{which}}
or \textit{\textbf{that}} in other cases. \\
Defining relative clauses identify nouns --- they tell us which person, thing
etc. the speaker means: \\
\textit{The girl \textbf{who} sits next to me is Japanese.} 
(\underline{no punctuation surround} this type of clause).\\\\
The relative pronoun can be omitted if it is the object of the clause: \\
\textit{The girl \textbf{(that) I spoke to} was Japanese.} \\\\
Non-defining relative clauses give \underline{extra information} about a
person or thing 
(This extra information is \underline{enclosed by commas}.): \\
\textit{Mr Brown, \textbf{who is an electrician}, mended our kitchen light.} \\\\

\textit{\textbf{Whose}} is used to \underline{indicate possession}: \\
\textit{My brother, \textbf{whose house} was burgled last week, has been asked to help with
police investigations.} \\\\
You can't use \textit{that} or omit the relative pronoun in non-defining relative clauses.\\

\subsection{The Passive}
The passive voice is formed by \underline{making the object of the active
  phrase} \\ \underline{into the subject of an new phrase}: \\
Active \textit{ --- \textbf{I wash} my car every week.} \\
Passive \textit{ --- My car \textbf{is} wash\textbf{ed} every week.} \\
Passive voice equivalents exist for all major active tenses. \\
The passive is used in three main ways: \\\\
\textbf{a)} To \underline{describe a process}.\\
\textbf{b)} When the \underline{action is more important} than who does it: \\
\textit{The photocopier \textbf{was} repair\textbf{ed} in two hours.} \\
\textbf{c)} When \underline{we do not know}, or do not want to say, \underline{who does the action}: \\
\textit{A window \textbf{has been broken}.} \\\\
The agent (\textit{by} ...) is only used when it includes important information: \\
\textit{America was discovered \textbf{by} Columbus in 1492.} \\
\underline{Never add "by someone" to a passive phrase.} \\\\

\begin{tabular}{l|l|l}
                    & \textbf{Active}                    & \textbf{Passive} \\ \hline
Present simple      & someone makes the pizza            & the pizza is made \\
Present continuous  & someone is making the pizza        & the pizza is being made \\
Present perfect     & someone has made the pizza         & the pizza has been made \\
Past simple         & someone made the pizza             & the pizza was made \\
Past continuous     & someone was making the pizza       & the pizza was being made \\
Past perfect        & someone had made the pizza         & the pizza had been made \\
\textit{Going to}   & someone is going to make the pizza & the pizza is going to be made \\
\textit{Will}       & someone will make the pizza        & the pizza will be made \\
\textit{Must}       & someone must make the pizza        & the pizza must be made \\
\textit{Have to}    & someone has to make the pizza      & the pizza has to be made \\
\end{tabular}

\subsection{\textit{Will/Going to} (Predictions)} 
When making predictions we can use \textit{will} or \textit{going to}. \\
\textit{\textbf{Will}} is used more \underline{to express the speaker's own opinion}: \\
\textit{I think \textbf{it'll be} a good party.} \\
\textit{\textbf{Going to}} is used when there is \underline{evidence of the future event}: \\
\textit{She's \textbf{going to have} a baby.} 

\subsection{Conditional 2}
Formed with \underline{\textit{If} + past tense, subject + \textit{would}}. \\
This is used to predict what would happen given an \underline{improbable} or \underline{hypothetical} situation. 
\textit{If I \textbf{were} a fish, I \textbf{would live} in the sea.} (But I'm not!) \\
Compare: \\
\textbf{a)} \textit{If it \textbf{rains, I'll take} an umbrella.} (first conditional.) \\
\textbf{b)} \textit{If I\textbf{ went, I wouldn't speak} to him. } (I'm probably not going.) 

\subsection{So/Nor} 
\textit{So} is used \underline{with modal and auxiliary verbs} to \underline{agree with positive statements}: \\
\textit{I like pizza. --- \textbf{So} do I.} \\
\textit{I can swim. --- \textbf{So} can she.} \\\\
\textit{Nor} is used \underline{to agree with negative statements}. \\
\textit{I don't like cabbage. --- \textbf{Nor} do I.} \\
\textit{I haven't done my homework. --- \textbf{Nor} has Marion.} \\\\
\textit{So/Nor} \underline{cannot be used to disagree with statements}: \\
\textit{I'm not going to the party. --- Really? I am.}

\subsection{Present perfect continuous}
The Present perfect continuous is \underline{used for activities}:\\\\
\textbf{a} which take place \underline{over an extended period} of time, or
which are \underline{repeated often} in an extended period of time:\\
\textit{I\textbf{'ve been}\ play\textbf{ing} tennis for five years }(regularly).\\
\textit{I\textbf{'ve been} play\textbf{ing} tennis all morning} (continuously). \\\\
\textbf{b} \underline{which began in the past} and either \underline{continue} or \underline{have finished in the present
time period} specified:\\
\textit{I\textbf{'ve been} writ\textbf{ing} letters today.} (Today has not finished.) \\
\textit{I\textbf{'ve been} read\textbf{ing} in English this morning.} (I have not had lunch yet.) \\
NB I am not necessarily reading or writing now. \\\\
\textbf{c} which are \underline{general rather than specific or counted}: \\
\textit{I\textbf{'ve been} listen\textbf{ing} to a lot of classical music lately.}\\
\subsection{Regrets (\textit{I wish}/\textit{if only})}
\underline{Past regrets} can be expressed by using \underline{\textit{I wish}/\textit{If only} + Past perfect}:\\\\
\textit{I went for a picnic. It rained.} (When? --- in the past). \\
\textit{I feel sad.} (Why? --- because it spoilt the picnic) = \\
\textbf{I wish} it\textbf{ had}n't rain\textbf{ed}. \\
\textbf{If only} it\textbf{ had}n't rain\textbf{ed}. \\
In this phrase we are expressing the fact that we \underline{would like to change} what
happened but \underline{it is impossible}, and so we feel sad. \\\\
\textit{\textbf{I wish} I \textbf{had}n't argu\textbf{ed} with my father.} \\
\textit{\textbf{If only} I \textbf{had}n't argu\textbf{ed} with my father.} \\
(But I did and I can't change it now; I'm sorry about it --- I regret it.) \\\\
\underline{\textit{I regret}} is followed by \underline{\textit{-ing}}, and is
normally used \underline{for events which are in our control}. 
It is a more formal way of expressing our feelings than \textit{I wish/If only}. \\

\subsection{Conditional 3}
This tense is formed with \underline{Past perfect, \textit{would} + \textit{have} + past participle}. 
It refers to\underline{ past time} --- \underline{to things that cannot now be changed}: \\\\
\textit{If it \textbf{had} rain\textbf{ed},} (Did it rain? --- No) \\
\textit{I \textbf{would have taken} an umbrella.} \\
(Did I take an umbrella? --- No. Why? --- because it didn't rain.) \\
\textit{If it \textbf{hadn't been} sunny,} (Was it sunny? --- Yes) \\
\textit{I \textbf{wouldn't have got sunburnt}.} (Did I get sunburnt? --- Yes.) \\\\

\textit{If \textbf{I'd seen} him, \textbf{I'd have said} hello.} \\\\
In writing we often contract \textit{had} and \textit{would} but not \textit{have} in third conditional

\subsection{Reported Statements}
Most tenses in direct speech \underline{change in report speech}: \\\\
\begin{tabular}{p{6,5cm}ll}
Present simple & --- & Past simple \\
Present perfect & --- & Past perfect \\
Present perfect continuous & --- & Past perfect continuous \\
Present continuous & --- & Past continuous \\
Past simple & --- & Past perfect \\
Past continuous & --- & Past perfect continuous \\
\end{tabular}\\\\
The Past perfect remains the same in reported speech. \\\\
Other similar forms change in similar ways:\\
\begin{tabular}{p{6,5cm}ll}
\textbf{am} going to & --- & \textbf{was} going to \\
\textbf{am} used to doing & --- & \textbf{was} used to doing \\
\textbf{have} to & --- & \textbf{had} to \\
\textbf{am} able & --- & \textbf{was} able \\
\textbf{have} something done & --- & \textbf{had} something done \\
\end{tabular}\\\\
There are occasions where \underline{you needn't change} the tense: \\
\textbf{a} where \underline{facts} are still true: \\
\textit{My name \textbf{is} Paul --- He said his name \textbf{is} Paul.} \\
\textbf{b} \underline{Past simple to Past perfect} depends on time references: \\
\textit{"Peter \textbf{left} when I arrived" --- She said Peter \textbf{left} when she arrived.} \\
(Time references are clear --- no need to change.) \\\\
\textit{"I \textbf{was} sick." --- She said she \textbf{had been} sick.} \\
(Here the Past simple (\textit{she was}) could be misinterpreted as representing the
Present simple ie \textit{she is sick now}.)\\\\
\underline{Modal verbs} can be divided into those that change and those that do not. \\\\
\begin{tabular}{p{6,5cm}ll}
\textbf{These can change:} && \textbf{These stay the same:} \\
can --- could && should \\
may --- might && could \\
will --- would && would \\
must --- had to && ought \\
shall --- should && might \\
\end{tabular}\\\\
\textit{Would} does not change to \textit{would have} in reported speech.

\textbf{a} \underline{\textit{Wh} questions}:\\

This type of question can be reported in full:\\

This form is used with certain verbs we are sure about the truth of the
information: \\
\textit{He is \textbf{said to be }living in the USA.} \\

reported to be \\

\subsection{Reporting verbs}
Often the meaning of a sentence in direct speech \underline{is summarised by a verb} in
reported speech. \\
\textit{"Would you like to go to the cinema, Jane?" \textbf{said} Eric. \\
Eric \textbf{invited} Jane to the cinema.} \\\\
There are three main types: \\\\
Type \textbf{A}: \underline{Verb + person + infinitive with "to"}. \\
\textit{He \textbf{told me to sit} down.}\\\\
Type \textbf{B}: \underline{Verb + infinitive with "to"}.\\
\textit{She \textbf{agreed to give} him a reference.} \\\\
Type \textbf{C}: \underline{Verb + -ing}. \\
\textit{She \textbf{regretted leaving} her job.} \\\\
\textit{He \textbf{apologised for losing} the report.} 

\subsection{\textit{It's (high) time} + subjunctive}
This form is used when something needs doing urgently: \\
\textit{\textbf{It's (high) time we went}.} (subjunctive) \\
(Adding "high" makes this form stronger.) \\
This mean we should have already gone --- it is stronger then \textit{It's time to
go}. \\\\
The subjunctive is formed in the same way as the past tense. \\

\section{Languages Focus}

\subsection{Question tags}
A question tag is a short interrogative phrase to a statement. \\
\textit{It's cold} (statement) \\
\textit{isn't it?} (tag). \\\\
Negative tags are added to positive statements and positive tags to negative
statements. \\
\textit{\textbf{It's not} cold, \textbf{is it}?} \\\\
The question tag repeats the auxiliary verb or modal auxiliary verb from the
statement, and the subject pronoun. 
If there is no auxiliary verb in the statement, \textit{do} is used: \\
\textit{You \textbf{like} oranges, \textbf{don't} you?} \\\\
There are exceptions, for example, imperatives: \\
\textit{Sit down, will you?} \\
\textit{Please help, won't you?} \\

\subsection{Future in the past}
\textit{These forms are used when we take a point in the past and talk about what
happened after that.} \\\\
\textit{When he was six, everyone knew \textbf{he was going to be} a footballer.} \\
\textit{He \textbf{went on to} play in the school team.} \\
\textit{He\textbf{ was to} appear in a total of thirty-four international matches before he
retired.} \\

\subsection{Uses of \textit{do}}
\textit{Do} is an auxiliary verb that is used to form questions and negatives. \\\\
\textit{\textbf{Do you know} the time?} \\
\textit{I'm sorry I \textbf{don't know} the time.} \\\\
\textit{Do} can be used to avoid repeating a longer phrase. \\
\textit{I don't like ice-cream, but my sister \textbf{does}.} \\\\
\textit{Do} is also used in positive statements to give emphasis to the verb. \\
\textit{Oh, I \textbf{do} like your new dress.} (Really, I'm not just being polite!) \\
\textit{I \textbf{do} work hard at school.} (I know you think I don't.) \\

\subsection{\textit{must --- have to}}
\underline{\textit{must}} \\
\textit{Must} is nearly only used in writing forms and in \underline{external obligations} by
law and in official language. 
\textit{Must} is used \underline{only in the present}. \\
\underline{Avoid to use \textit{must} in personal speech!} \\ 
\textit{You \textbf{mustn't} smoke here.} \\
\underline{\textit{have to}} \\
\textit{Have to} is used \underline{in personal speech}. \\
\textit{I \textbf{have to} get up early everyday.} \\
In the \underline{past} you can use only \textit{had to} (\textit{must} is not
possible): \\
\textit{When I was a child, I \textbf{had to} wear a uniform at school.} \\
